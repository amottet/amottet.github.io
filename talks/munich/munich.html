Complexity Theory

For years when I taught algorithms or complexity, I would tell my students that while there is an infinite range of intermediate complexity classes, problems occurring in practice tend to be complete for one of a handful of important complexity classes.
I used to say that no one knows why this happens.
Now however I can say that it happens for an algebraic reason which [CSP people] are close to figuring out.

Neil Immerman, 2014

Constraint Languages

Parametrize the problem by a constraint language $\mathbb A=(A;R_1,\dots,R_k)$ consisting of:
  • a fixed set $A$, the domain,
  • a number of relations on $A$.
$\CSP(\mathbb A)$: decide satisfiability of an instance whose constraints all belong to the constraint language.
  • $\CSP(\{0,1\};\text{all relations of arity } 3)$: 3-SAT
  • $\CSP(\{0,1\};\neq)$: 2-coloring $$x\neq y, y\neq z, z\neq t, t\neq y$$
  • $\CSP(\mathbb N;<)$: directed acyclicity

Chapter 2
Islands of Tractability

Algebra as a source of tractability

Given solutions $h_1,\dots,h_n\colon V\to A$, can one infer the existence of other solutions?
A map $\varphi\colon A^n\to A$ is a polymorphism of $\mathbb A$ if for all instances of $\CSP(\mathbb A)$ and all possible solutions $h_1,\dots,h_n\colon V\to A$, the map $x\mapsto \varphi(h_1(x),\dots,h_n(x))$ is a solution.
  • $(x_1,\dots,x_n)\mapsto x_i$ works
  • if $\varphi,\psi_1,\dots,\psi_m$ are polymorphisms, then $\varphi\circ(\psi_1,\dots,\psi_m)$ is a polymorphism

$\leadsto$ Space of solutions has an algebraic structure

When $\mathbb A$ is finite, tractability is found precisely when $\mathbb A$ has non-trivial polymorphisms.
Suppose that $\mathbb A$ is finite. The following are equivalent (if $\text{P}\neq\text{NP}$):
  • $\mathbb A$ does not logically define 3-SAT
  • $\CSP(\mathbb A)$ is solvable in polynomial timeBulatov [FOCS'17] // Zhuk [FOCS'17, J. ACM'20]
  • $\mathbb A$ has a polymorphism $\varphi\colon A^n\to A$ satisfyingBarto, Kozik [LMCS'12] $$\forall \overline x\in A^n: \varphi(x_1,\dots,x_n)=\varphi(x_2,\dots,x_n,x_1)$$
Suppose that $\mathbb A$ is finite. The following are equivalent:
  • $\mathbb A$ does not logically define linear equations over finite fields
  • $\CSP(\mathbb A)$ is solvable by local consistency / linear programming / semidefinite programmingBarto, Kozik [J. ACM'14]
  • $\CSP(\mathbb A)$ is definable in fixpoint logic (with or without counting)Atserias, Bulatov, Dawar [Theor. Comput. Sci. '09]
  • Maróti, McKenzie [Algebra Univ. '08]For all $n\geq 3$, $\mathbb A$ has a polymorphism $\varphi\colon A^n\to A$ satisfying$$\forall x,y\in A: \varphi(y,x,\dots,x)=\varphi(x,y,\dots,x)=\dots=\varphi(x,\dots,x,y)$$
Suppose that $\mathbb A$ is finite. The following are equivalent:
  • $\CSP(\mathbb A)$ is solvable in first-order logic
  • $\mathbb A$ has a tolerant polymorphismLarose, Loten, Tardif [LMCS'07]
  • $\mathbb A$ has finite dualityAtserias [Europ. J. Comb. '08]
  • Tractability is found whenever the problem has interesting symmetries.
  • Better symmetries imply more easily solvable.
  • Few symmetries imply a logical richness and computational hardness.

Logical Separations of Finite CSPs

Problem: Given two finite constraint languages $\mathbb A,\mathbb B$, does there exist a first-order sentence $\Phi$ such that $\CSP(\mathbb A)\subseteq\operatorname{Mod}(\Phi)\subseteq\CSP(\mathbb B)$.
Motivation: efficient solvability of finding solutions for restricted instances of $\CSP(\mathbb B)$ $\Rightarrow$ Promise CSPs
M. [CSL'24] Larrauri, M. [24+]
The following are equivalent:
  • There exists such a $\Phi$,
  • There exists a finite constraint language $\mathbb C$ such that $\CSP(\mathbb A)\subseteq\CSP(\mathbb C)\subseteq\CSP(\mathbb B)$ and $\CSP(\mathbb C)$ is first-order definable.
Given $\mathbb A,\mathbb B$, one can decide whether such a $\Phi$ exists.
The proof uses:
  • Characterization: techniques from infinite-domain CSPs
  • Decidability: techniques from automata theory

Conclusion

  • A very expressive class of computational problems Gillibert, Jonušas, Kompatscher, M., Pinsker [SICOMP'22]
  • A rule-of-thumb characterizing problems that are efficiently solvable, strong interplay between logic and algebra
  • Strong connections with artificial intelligence that allow to solve "old" open problems
    (query rewriting, but also complexity of reasoning in spatial and temporal formalisms)Bodirsky, Jonsson, Martin, M., Semanišinová [SICOMP'24]
  • New algorithmic techniques that can potentially help to solve practical problems and problems interesting in theory AND in practice (parity games, mean-payoff games)
  • Many extensions of CSPs related to central problems in complexity theory (Unique Games Conjecture, hardness of approximation)
